# Surf slang

A guide to language that surfers use. This is as much for my own memory as it is for anyone else.

Think something is wrong? [Open a pull request](). 

## A

#### A-frame
A wave that breaks symmetrically from the [peak](#peak) in both directions at once, forming a shape like the letter A. An A-frame offers both a [left](#left--left-hander) and a right, giving two surfers the opportunity to take off simultaneously — one going each way — without [dropping in](#drop-in-verb--drop-in-noun) on each other.

#### Air / Aerial
A manoeuvre where the surfer launches off the [lip](#lip) of the wave and becomes airborne before landing back on the wave.

#### Ankle biters / Ankle busters
Very small, weak waves that barely break above ankle height.

## B

#### Back paddle
To paddle around a surfer to get closer to the [peak](#peak) and claim priority over someone who has been waiting longer. A direct synonym of [Snake](#snake), though *back paddle* describes the physical action more literally.

#### Backdoor
To take off behind the [peak](#peak) and surf through a [hollow](#hollow) [section](#section)/[barrel](#barrel).

#### Backside / Backhand
Surfing with your back facing the wave. Opposite of [Frontside](#frontside).

#### Bail
To deliberately jump off your board to avoid a [wipeout](#wipeout) or collision. See also [Eject](#eject), which is used more interchangeably but can imply a more last-second, forced abandonment.

#### Barrel
A [hollow](#hollow), tube-shaped [section](#section) formed when a wave pitches over. See also [Tube](#tube) and [Pit](#pit).

#### Beach break
Waves that break over a sandy bottom. Small beach breaks can be less hazardous than [reef breaks](#reef-break) because of the soft bottom. However some beach breaks can be fast and [steep](#steep). The [peaks](#peak) on a beach break can be unpredictable and change from one day to the next as the [sandbanks](#sandbank) shift around.

#### Blown out
Conditions where [onshore](#onshore) or [cross-shore](#cross-shore) winds have made the wave surface choppy and messy, making surfing more difficult or poor quality because the [peak](#peak) is hard to determine and/or not [lined-up](#lined-up).

#### Bomb
An exceptionally large wave in a [set](#set), notably bigger than the rest.

#### Bottom turn
The foundational manoeuvre where a surfer turns at the base of the wave to redirect their momentum up the [face](#face).

#### Bowl
A section of wave where the seafloor rises sharply, causing the wave to jack up and bend into a concave, bowl-like shape. Bowls create extra speed and steepness, often producing [hollow](#hollow), [barreling](#barrel) sections. Surfers seek them out for turns and tubes but they can be unforgiving.

#### Burn
To deliberately [drop in](#drop-in-verb--drop-in-noun) on another surfer. The distinction matters: a [drop-in](#drop-in-verb--drop-in-noun) can happen accidentally through poor awareness or misjudgement, whereas being *burned* implies the other surfer did it on purpose. "He burned me on the best wave of the day."

#### A burger
A [fat](#fat), slow, weak wave — the kind that looks promising but offers little to work with.

## C

#### Carve
A powerful, arcing turn that uses the full [rail](#rail) of the board to redirect across the wave [face](#face).

#### Caught inside
Being trapped between the breaking waves and the shore, making it difficult to paddle back out to the [lineup](#lineup).

#### Charge
To fearlessly commit to large or dangerous waves. A surfer who takes on big waves is called a *charger*. See also [Send it](#send-it), which shares this spirit but implies slightly less experience or certainty.

#### Clean up set
A large, unexpected [set](#set) that arrives and breaks further outside than normal, catching most surfers in the [lineup](#lineup) off guard and [inside](#inside). A clean up set effectively clears the lineup — hence the name — as everyone is caught inside and held down or pushed to shore.

#### Closeout
When an entire wave or a large [section](#section) of a wave breaks all at once along its entire length, leaving no open [face](#face) to surf.

#### Crest
The top of a wave. See also [Lip](#lip), which specifically refers to the top edge as it begins to pitch and throw.

#### Cross-shore
Wind blowing across the waves at an angle, neither directly [onshore](#onshore) nor [offshore](#offshore). Cross-shore winds can make conditions messy and choppy — contributing to [blown out](#blown-out) surf — though they are generally less damaging than fully onshore winds.

#### Critical
Describing a moment in a wave or a maneuver that is right on the edge of what is possible — where the margin for error is tiny. A critical [take off](#take-off) is one where the surfer barely makes it to their feet before the wave throws over. A critical [section](#section) is one that's about to [closeout](#closeout) and requires immediate action. Surfing critically or surfing critical waves is considered a higher level of performance.

## D

#### Deep
Being positioned close to the [peak](#peak) — or behind it — when [taking off](#take-off). The deeper you are, the more committed and [critical](#critical) your position on the wave. Being *too deep* means you've taken off too far behind or close to the peak and the wave may outrun you, leaving you stuck in the [lip](#lip) rather than on the open [face](#face).

#### Deck
The top surface of a surfboard where the surfer stands.

#### Doggy door
An escape route out of the back of a [barrel](#barrel) — where the surfer exits through a small opening at the shoulder of the wave rather than coming out through the front. Named for the small flap a dog uses to get in and out of a house. A doggy door exit is sometimes the only option when the barrel closes out in front of you.

#### Drop in (verb) / Drop-in (noun)
To catch a wave that another surfer is already riding, cutting them off and therefore reducing the length of ride that they could have had. Often dangerous as the surfer you've dropped in on may collide with you. Very bad surf etiquette. See also [Burn](#burn), which implies the act was intentional, and [Snake](#snake), which is the act of positioning to steal priority before the wave is even caught.

## E

#### Eject
To jump off the board, usually at the last moment to avoid a dangerous [wipeout](#wipeout). See [Bail](#bail) — the terms are near-synonyms, but *eject* implies a more urgent, forced exit.

## F

#### Face
The open, unbroken surface of a wave that a surfer rides across.

#### Fade / Faded take off
A take off in which the surfer initially angles their [take off](#take-off) *away* from the open [face](#face) — toward the breaking part of the wave — before snapping back into a [bottom turn](#bottom-turn) and redirecting down the line. A faded take off sets the surfer up for a more [critical](#critical), powerful [bottom turn](#bottom-turn) and a deeper, more committed [line](#line).

#### Fat
A wave (or section of a wave) that is wide, slow, and lacking in steepness or power. A fat wave doesn't pitch or throw and gives the surfer little to work with — there's not enough slope to generate speed and no [lip](#lip) to hit. The opposite of [steep](#steep). Fat waves are also less likely to [barrel](#barrel). That said, fat waves are often better for beginners and [longboarders](#longboard) — the gentle, forgiving slope gives you more time to [pop up](#pop-up) and find your feet, and a [longboard's](#longboard) volume means it can generate enough speed on a [mushy](#mushy) wave to still have fun. See also [A burger](#a-burger).

#### Firing
Excellent surf conditions / good waves.

#### Fish
A short, wide, twin-fin or quad-fin surfboard design characterised by a swallowtail and a lot of volume through the middle. Fishes are designed to surf fast and loose in smaller, [mushier](#mushy) waves that a [shortboard](#shortboard) would struggle in. They sacrifice some control in larger surf for speed and flow in weaker conditions.

#### Flat
No waves. The ocean surface is calm with no surfable swell.

#### Flow
A state of surfing in which movement feels effortless, connected, and intuitive — where the surfer is reacting to the wave instinctively rather than thinking about each manoeuvre. Flow is the aesthetic ideal of surfing, particularly associated with [longboarding](#longboard), [midlength](#midlength) and [glidier](#glider) styles of surfing. A surfer with flow looks unhurried even when moving fast, threading each [section](#section) smoothly and making the wave look easy.

#### Floater
A manoeuvre where the surfer rides up onto the breaking [lip](#lip) of a wave and glides across the white water before dropping back down. Can be used to extend a ride by going over an area of white water onto another open [face](#face) — where the surfer can then continue their ride.

#### Foamball
The white water and turbulence at the base of a breaking wave inside a [barrel](#barrel).

#### Foamie
A surfboard construction in which the entire surfboard is built with foam. No fibreglass is used in the construction and therefore quite soft and forgiving if your body hits it. All foamies are [softtops](#softtop) because they have a soft deck — not all [softtops](#softtop) are foamies.

#### Frothing
Being intensely excited about surfing — often to the point of barely being able to contain it. You might be frothing about an upcoming swell, or just frothing to get in the water. Closely related to [Stoke](#stoke--stoked) but more visceral and immediate. "The swell forecast is pumping — I'm absolutely frothing."

#### Frontside
Surfing with your chest and face toward the wave. Opposite of [Backside](#backside--backhand). Generally considered easier to perform most manoeuvres and therefore surfers will often prefer a wave that is breaking in the direction that they can surf frontside (right if you're [regular-footed](#regular-foot) and left if you're [goofy-footed](#goofy-foot)).

## G

#### Getting shacked
Being fully inside a [barrel](#barrel) — riding through the [hollow](#hollow) tube of a breaking wave. Synonymous with getting [tubed](#tube), [pitted](#pit), or [barrelled](#barrel), though *shacked* is more colloquial and enthusiastic in tone. "I got absolutely shacked on that one." One of the most celebrated experiences in surfing.

#### Glassy
Wave conditions where the surface of the water is completely smooth, like glass — usually the result of calm winds or light [offshore](#offshore) conditions. Glassy surf is widely considered ideal. The opposite of [textured](#textured).

#### Glider
A long, high-volume longboard — often 9'6" and above — designed for effortless wave-catching and graceful, flowing riding. Gliders are not built for aggressive turns; they excel at trimming, and [flow](#flow).

#### Goofy foot
A surfer who rides with their right foot forward. Opposite of [Regular foot](#regular-foot).

#### Grom
A young surfer, typically a teenager or younger. It carries no negative connotation and is widely used as a neutral or even endearing term for talented young surfers. Groms will often surf on the [inside](#inside), because they are able to increase their wave count by being small and nimble on [steeper](#steep) waves.

#### Groveller
A short, wide, thick surfboard with a lot of volume relative to its length, designed to perform in small, [weak](#mushy) surf. A groveller gets the surfer onto waves quickly and generates speed in conditions where a standard [shortboard](#shortboard) would feel sluggish. The trade-off is reduced responsiveness in larger, more powerful surf.

#### Hybrid
A surfboard that blends design elements from two or more board types — typically combining the paddle power and looseness of a [fish](#fish) or [groveller](#groveller) with the performance characteristics of a [shortboard](#shortboard). Hybrids are versatile all-rounders, suited to a wide range of conditions.

## H

#### Hang five / Hang ten
[Noseriding](#noseride) moves in longboarding where the surfer walks to the [nose](#nose) and hangs five toes (one foot) or ten toes (both feet) off the front of the board.

#### Head dip
When a surfer leans their head into the [barrel](#barrel) wall while attempting tube riding, making contact with the wave. A head dip is sometimes considered a less good, 'mini barrel'.

#### Hold-down
Being held underwater by the force of a breaking wave after a [wipeout](#wipeout), unable to surface.

#### Hollow
A wave (or section of wave) where the [lip](#lip) throws out ahead of the [face](#face), creating an air pocket — or [barrel](#barrel) — inside the breaking wave. Hollow waves require a steep, fast [take off](#take-off) and are both the most exciting and most dangerous type of wave to surf. The degree of hollowness is linked to the shape of the seafloor beneath: abrupt, shallow [reef breaks](#reef-break) and [sandbanks](#sandbank) tend to produce the most hollow waves. The opposite of [fat](#fat) or [mushy](#mushy).

## I

#### Inside
The area closer to shore where waves are breaking, or the position of a surfer closer to shore relative to others. Being *caught inside* (see [Caught inside](#caught-inside)) is a consequence of being in this zone during a large [set](#set). As waves break closer and often [steeper](#steep) on the inside, you may find surfers on shorter boards here because they need a steeper wave to [take off](#take-off).

## J

#### Jacking
When a wave rapidly increases in size and steepness as it hits shallow water. A wave that *jacks* hard creates a more intense, often [hollow](#hollow) [barrel](#barrel).

## K

#### Kick out
Deliberately ending a ride by turning over the back of the wave. A controlled exit from a wave.

#### Kook
An all-too-common term of disrespect in surf culture which people should really stop using. Many will claim that being called a kook implies not just inexperience but a failure to respect surf culture or etiquette and therefore it's a valid insult to apply to some surfers. But in reality it is just a synonym for "surfer I deem to be lesser than I".

## L

#### Left / Left-hander
A wave that breaks from right to left from the surfer's perspective as they ride toward shore.

#### Line
The path along the [face](#face) of the wave that a surfer chooses to ride. Reading the wave well means picking the right line — threading through [sections](#section), staying in the [pocket](#pocket), or setting up for a [barrel](#barrel). "He found a beautiful line through that [closeout](#closeout) section."

#### Lined-up
A descriptor for a wave (or set of waves) that is breaking in a clean, orderly, and predictable fashion, offering a well-defined [face](#face) and [pocket](#pocket). Similar to [Walled-up](#walled-up) — see that entry for the nuance. Not to be confused with [Lineup](#lineup), which refers to the area where surfers wait.

#### Lineup
The area just beyond the breaking waves where surfers sit and wait for incoming [sets](#set). It can be used to describe both the general area ("I sit and wait for waves in the lineup") *as well as* the collection of surfers present ("The lineup was really friendly today").

#### Lip
The very top edge of a wave as it begins to pitch forward and break. More specific than [Crest](#crest) — the lip is dynamic, the crest is simply the top of the wave.

#### Longboard
A surfboard typically 9 feet or longer, with a rounded nose and a lot of volume. Longboards are the easiest type of board to paddle and catch waves on, making them popular with beginners — but they are also their own artform, with skills like [noseriding](#noseride), cross-stepping, and [hanging ten](#hang-five--hang-ten) being the pinnacle of longboard style. Compare with [Shortboard](#shortboard), [Midlength](#midlength), and [Glider](#glider).

#### Lull
A period between [sets](#set) when the ocean is calm and no waves are arriving. Opposite of a [Set](#set).

## M

#### Macking
Describing very large, powerful surf. Slightly more intense than [Firing](#firing) — *macking* almost always refers specifically to size.

#### Midlength
A surfboard in the 7–9 foot range that sits between a [shortboard](#shortboard) and a [longboard](#longboard) in terms of length, volume, and feel. Midlengths are increasingly popular because they offer easier wave-catching than a shortboard while remaining more manoeuvrable than a full longboard. A broad category that includes [mini-mals](#mini-mal) and various single-fin and twin-fin designs.

#### Mini-mal
Short for *mini-malibu*. A beginner-friendly [midlength](#midlength) board, typically 7–8 feet, with a rounded nose and generous volume. The mini-mal is the most common learn-to-surf board because it is stable, floaty, and easy to paddle. As surfers progress, they typically move to shorter, thinner boards.

#### Morning sickness
A phenomenon where the surf appears clean and glassy in the early morning but the waves are actually weak, disorganised, or lacking in power — despite looking great. Often caused by a dropping swell or deceptive light conditions at dawn. You paddle out for what looks like a beautiful [dawn patrol](#dawn-patrol) and find there's nothing to ride.

#### Mushy
Soft, slow, weak waves that lack power or steepness. The opposite of [hollow](#hollow) or [steep](#steep). Mushy waves can still be fun — especially on a [longboard](#longboard), [fish](#fish), or [groveller](#groveller) — but they don't lend themselves to powerful turns or [barrels](#barrel).

## N

#### Nose
The front tip of a surfboard.

#### Noseride
A longboarding manoeuvre where the surfer walks to the front of the board and rides from the [nose](#nose). See [Hang five / Hang ten](#hang-five--hang-ten).

#### Nugs
Waves.

## O

#### Offshore
Wind blowing from the land out to sea. This grooms waves and holds them up, creating cleaner surf with a better [face](#face). A gentle offshore is often considered ideal conditions.

#### Onshore
Wind blowing from the sea toward land. Makes waves choppy and messy. See [Blown out](#blown-out).

#### Outside
The area further out to sea, beyond where the waves are breaking. Calling *"outside!"* can be a warning that a large [set](#set) is approaching. The longer and/or more buoyant your surfboard, the easier it is to paddle and therefore you are better equipped to catch a wave as it forms outside.

## P

#### Party wave
When multiple surfers catch and ride the same wave together, usually in a fun, non-competitive spirit. *Consent is needed when taking a party wave* — otherwise it is considered a [drop-in](#drop-in-verb--drop-in-noun) (not good!).

#### Pearl / Pearling
When the [nose](#nose) of the board dips under the water during [take off](#take-off), causing the surfer to be launched forward. Also called a *nosedive*.

#### Peak
The highest point of a wave where it first begins to break, from which it peels in both directions.

#### Pit
The deepest, most intense part of the [barrel](#barrel). A synonym of [Barrel](#barrel) and [Tube](#tube), but *pit* specifically evokes the dark, heavy, enclosed quality of a serious tube.

#### Pocket
The steepest, most powerful part of the wave on the [face](#face) but *just* ahead of the breaking white-water. This is where a surfer wants/needs to be 90% of the time because it is where they can generate the most speed (needed for turns) or get the most hold on a longboard for making [noserides](#noseride).

#### Point break
Waves that break and wrap along a headland, typically producing long, consistent, predictable rides.

#### Pop up
The motion of jumping from a prone paddling position to a standing position on the surfboard. The [pop up](#pop-up) is *part* of the [take off](#take-off) process — the take off encompasses the whole act of catching and committing to the wave, of which the pop up is the physical movement.

## R

#### Rail
The edge/side of a surfboard. Surfers engage their rails when [carving](#carve) or turning.

#### Reef break
Waves that break over a coral or rock reef. Often produces more consistent surf than a [beach break](#beach-break), but with more hazardous consequences for [wipeouts](#wipeout).

#### Regular foot
A surfer who rides with their left foot forward. Opposite of [Goofy foot](#goofy-foot).

#### Ripping
Surfing with exceptional skill and aggression, executing powerful, precise manoeuvres. See also [Shredding](#shred--shredding), a near-synonym — *ripping* often implies cleaner, more intentional technique, while *shredding* implies wild, energetic destruction of the wave.

#### Rocker
The curvature of a surfboard from [nose](#nose) to [tail](#tail) when viewed from the side.

## S

#### Sandbank
A raised area of sand on the seabed, shaped by currents and tides, that causes waves to break in a particular spot. Good sandbanks are essential for quality [beach break](#beach-break) surf — they create defined [peaks](#peak) and predictable [lines](#line). Sandbanks shift constantly, which is why beach break conditions can change dramatically from week to week.

#### Section
A portion of a wave that breaks or changes character ahead of the surfer. Surfers try to beat or navigate sections.

#### Send it
To fully commit to a wave despite not being 100% confident about it — often in heavier or larger surf than you're accustomed to. Analogous to [Charge](#charge), but *send it* implies a bit more recklessness or bravado; a charger is experienced in big waves, whereas someone who *sends it* might be punching above their weight. "The [set](#set) wave was twice overhead and I just sent it."

#### Session
A single outing in the water for surfing.

#### Set
A group of waves arriving together, usually larger than the background swell. Preceded by a [Lull](#lull).

#### Shortboard
A high-performance surfboard typically between 5'6" and 6'6", thin, narrow, with a pointed nose and a relatively low volume. Shortboards are designed for powerful, critical surfing in good waves but require skill and strong paddling to use effectively. Contrast with [Longboard](#longboard), [Midlength](#midlength), [Fish](#fish), and [Groveller](#groveller).

#### Shred / Shredding
Surfing aggressively and skilfully. See [Ripping](#ripping) for nuance — *shredding* evokes more chaotic, explosive energy.

#### Slab
An extremely thick, [hollow](#hollow), and powerful wave that breaks over a very shallow reef or rock shelf. Slabs are among the most dangerous waves in surfing — they [jack](#jacking) up suddenly, producing short, incredibly intense barrels that detonate in just a few feet of water. Not for the faint-hearted.

#### Snake
To paddle around a surfer and get closer to the [peak](#peak) in order to claim priority over the surfer who has been waiting their turn longer. See also [Back paddle](#back-paddle), which is a direct synonym describing the same action from a physical perspective.

#### Softtop
A surfboard construction in which the deck of the surfboard is soft foam instead of exposed fibreglass. Some softtops are entirely free of fibreglass and called [foamies](#foamie) — whereas other softtops are basically a fibreglass-coated surfboard with an *additional* layer of soft foam on the deck.

#### Soul session
Surfing on your own.

#### Stall
A technique used to deliberately slow down on the wave. Most commonly associated with [barrel](#barrel) riding — where the surfer stalls to let the [tube](#tube) catch up with them — but also used in [longboarding](#longboard) to set up a [noseride](#noseride), allowing the wave to do the work of pushing the board forward while the surfer walks to the [nose](#nose). A stall can be performed by shifting weight to the [tail](#tail), dragging a hand in the water, or dropping a knee.

#### Steep
A wave (or section of wave) with a sharply angled [face](#face) — near vertical. Steep waves demand a fast, committed [take off](#take-off) and allow for powerful turns, but they also increase the risk of [pearling](#pearl--pearling). Steeper waves are more likely to produce [hollow](#hollow), [barreling](#barrel) sections. The opposite of [fat](#fat) or [mushy](#mushy). Closely related to [Jacking](#jacking), which describes the process of a wave becoming steep rapidly.

#### Stoke / Stoked
The feeling of joy, excitement, and passion associated with surfing and ocean culture.

## T

#### Tail
The back end of a surfboard. Different tail shapes (squash, pin, swallow, etc.) affect how the board performs.

#### Take off
The act of catching a wave and getting to your feet — the moment of commitment that starts a ride. As a noun: "That was a late take off." As a verb: "He took off behind the [peak](#peak)." The [pop up](#pop-up) is the physical movement that is *part* of the take off, but the take off as a whole includes reading the wave, paddling into it, and committing to the drop.

#### Textured
Waves or water surface that is slightly bumpy and rough, caused by wind blowing across it. Textured surf is less ideal than [glassy](#glassy) conditions but is often still very surfable — particularly with [offshore](#offshore) or light [cross-shore](#cross-shore) wind. The opposite of [glassy](#glassy).

#### Tombstoning
When a surfer is [held underwater](#hold-down) by their leash after a [wipeout](#wipeout), causing the board to stand vertically at the surface — a visual sign that the surfer is deep underwater.

#### Tube
The [hollow](#hollow), cylindrical space inside a breaking wave. Interchangeable with [Barrel](#barrel) in most contexts, though *tube* is often used in a more technical sense (e.g., *tube riding*, *getting tubed*).

## W

#### Wall
A long, open, unbroken section of a wave [face](#face) that allows for extended riding.

#### Walled-up
A wave (or set of waves) that is [lined-up](#lined-up) and presenting a long, open [face](#face) — ready to be ridden. Used as a synonym of [Lined-up](#lined-up), though *walled-up* tends to emphasise the visual impression of the wave standing up tall in front of you, while *lined-up* is more about the organisation and predictability of the break.

#### Wedge
A wave formed by two swells combining or a reflection bouncing off a jetty, creating an extra-[steep](#steep), powerful [peak](#peak).

#### Whitewater
The foamy broken water after a wave has crashed.

#### Wipeout
Falling off your board while surfing. The universal term for any surfing fall.
